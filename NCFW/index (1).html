about us

No Country For Women is a national campaign to change the problematic attitude of a country toward half of its population. It fights problems of gender-based violence and discrimination through:

Education

Conducting workshops in schools and colleges.

Conversation

Distributing written and audio-visual material through social and mass media channels to spark informed conversations about social change.

Action

Funding and promoting proposals for effective long-term solutions.

Statistics

Gender-based violence in India - quantified.

Every 22 minutes, one woman gets raped in India.
From 1971 to 2011, cases of rape increased by 873 percent.
Three out of four accused in the crime are acquitted.
Every 76 minutes, one child gets raped in India.
Only 1 in 600 Delhi rape cases in 2012 led to conviction.
Conviction rate fell from 41% to 24% in the past 12 years.

Our Philosophy

No Country For Women strives to develop effective long-term solutions that combat the sociocultural and institutional frameworks that perpetuate, sanction and pardon rape and rape culture. We employ a "D3" philosophy: Deconstruct. Dismantle. Develop.

  • Deconstruct
  • Dismantle
  • Develop

The act of rape is not merely an act of sexual violence - it is also a cultural, social, political and economic phenomena. The problem of rape cannot be understood in isolation from the culture and convictions of the society within which it occurs. Through our "D3" philosophy, we hope to provide a framework to understand its roots. Click on each tenet to read about what it stands for.

Deconstruct

de·con·struct
Verb. To analyze a conceptual system and to expose its hidden assumptions.
Sociocultural, political and economic institutions are deconstructed to evaluate the ways in which they condone gender-based discrimination and violence.

Dismantle

dis·man·tle
Verb. To take a structure to pieces.
Problematic sociocultural frameworks are dismantled by active resistance on behalf of the individual and community.

Develop

de·vel·op
Verb. To bring into being.
Effective long-term solutions are developed to tackle the problem of rape by tackling the institutions that sanction it; treating both - symptom and cause.

background

In December 2012, lndia shook. Statistics previously met with inattentive dismissal were rapidly gaining tangibility. The Delhi Rape incident was not an isolated example of brutality, it was a manifestation of the deep-rooted misogynistic beliefs that pervade Indian culture and society. The prevalence of rape in India goes beyond mere structural issues such as poverty and unsafe neighborhoods; it is the product of problematic mindsets that blame the victim for the act of violence.

Despite this, most efforts to tackle the issue of rape in India fail to take into account the societal sanctioning of rape. What India needs is a sustainable anti-rape movement that addresses both symptom and cause by tackling the social institutions that create such mindsets. We hope to meet this goal by initiating discourse about the various institutions and environments that perpetuate problematic mindsets, while facilitating solutions that combat both rape as well as rape culture.

We hope to create agents and activists - agents that are empowered and recognize that they need not necessarily conform to societal roles and expectations, activists that will spread what they learned from us to their peers and use their deep understanding of the issues to kickstart effective long-term solutions. Thus, to achieve this, we plan to:

  • Hold educational workshops in schools and colleges.
  • Raise awareness about sociocultural causes of rape through social & mass media.
  • Encourage the youth to develop effective long-term solutions.
  • Fund and promote proposed solutions.

Our efforts are being supported by notable collaboration and contribution from Projects for Peace , Transhuman Collective , YourStory and Brown University .

Donate

No Country for Women has been supported by Projects for Peace and Brown University. Our workshops, conference and audio-visual content have been immensely successful and well-received. We have been extended numerous invites from various top institutions and bodies - including but not limited to numerous IITs, IIMs, the Harvard-India initiative, TEDx LSR, and schools across the country. We have been featured in national and international media; our videos and visual content have received over a million views.

We have plans of expansion to maximize our impact and leverage our momentum to effect lasting social change. Post-September, we plan to expand to schools, colleges and institutions in New Delhi, Mumbai and Hyderabad. We have also been asked to partner with Social Development and Women’s Development Cells of organizations all over India and are in the process of developing long-term relationships with research institutions, NGOs and within the education secton.
